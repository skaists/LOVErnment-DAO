# D-3 — Is a decision circle of 8 supported by the research? (no advocacy)

**Question.** The skaists circle size is founder-ruled at **8**, rationale "optimal decision-group
size." This memo reports what the decision-science literature actually supports for decision
*quality*, including where it disagrees with 8. It takes no side; it determines only whether 8
ships **with citations** (as research-optimal) or **marked founder-ruled** (a defensible choice
the literature does not center on).

## What the literature supports (author / year / finding)
- **Hackman & Vidmar (1970)** — across tasks with groups of 2–7, the point where members stopped
  saying the group was "too small / too big" **intersected at ≈4.6 members** → commonly rounded to
  **~5** as the optimum. *(Pinned: ingbrief summary; metalogue.co.uk.)*
- **J.R. Hackman (Harvard, later work)** — **member satisfaction declines as team size grows**;
  teams of **≤6 tend to draw on most members and perform better**; practical optimum **4–6**, and
  **the largest a team should be is ~10**. *(Pinned: metalogue.co.uk.)*
- **Katzenbach & Smith, *The Wisdom of Teams* (1993)** — from 50 teams / 30+ organizations: teams
  should be **"small enough to meet frequently and be interactive"**; if more people are needed,
  **form sub-teams**. (Their broader finding, widely cited: most effective teams are **well under
  ~10**.) *(Pinned: Warwick Business School; Praxis Framework.)*
- **Bezos / Amazon "two-pizza rule"** — a team no larger than **~6–8** people. *(Pinned:
  myshyft.com; ilms.academy.)* — this is the band that **includes 8 at its upper edge**.
- **McKinsey (2024)** — **small teams decide ~25% faster** than large ones. *(Pinned: metalogue
  citing McKinsey.)*
- **Systematic review — "Size and Decision-making" (2022)** — literature review across groups/teams
  confirming the small-group tendency. *(Pinned: ResearchGate 359046455.)*

## Where the literature disagrees with 8
1. **The central optimum is ~5, not 8.** Hackman & Vidmar's 4.6 and Hackman's 4–6 both sit
   **below** 8. The single strongest number the evidence supports is **~5**; 8 is above it.
2. **8 is at/just past the upper edge of "small."** It fits the two-pizza band (6–8) and stays
   under Hackman's ~10 ceiling, so it is **defensible but not optimal** — the tail of the supported
   range, not its center.
3. **Even-size deadlock.** 8 is **even**; a ranking/consensus circle of 8 can split 4–4. To avoid
   this it needs a **supermajority rule** (the parent system ƒractally uses **4/6** for exactly
   this reason; an 8-circle would need e.g. **6/8**). An **odd** size (**7**) avoids the tiebreak
   problem outright. *(Mechanical observation; ƒractally 4/6 pinned in D-1.)*
4. **Coordination cost grows ~n².** Pairwise links = n(n−1)/2 → **5 people = 10 links, 8 = 28**.
   Social-loafing / Ringelmann effects rise with size. *(General finding across the above sources.)*
5. **Nearest governance precedents chose smaller.** ƒractally uses **6**; Eden's decision groups
   target small recursive groups (D-2). The closest analogues to skaists picked **≤6**, i.e.
   **below 8**. *(Pinned: D-1, D-2.)*

## One counter-current worth recording (keeps this honest)
- **Online/large-group collective intelligence** — "Is bigger better? … collective intelligence in
  online groups" (2022) finds **larger online groups can raise collective intelligence**, i.e. the
  classic ~5 optimum was measured mostly on **in-person** groups and may shift for **online /
  asynchronous** settings. If skaists circles are online, the strict "≤6" ceiling is **less
  settled**. *(Pinned: ScienceDirect S0167923622001853.)*
- **Caveat on a commonly-misused citation:** **Miller's "7±2" (1956)** is about **working-memory
  span**, **not** decision-group quality — it should **not** be cited to justify 8. *(Standard
  correction; flagged so it isn't used as false support.)*

## Determination (what this memo decides, stated without advocacy)
- The decision-quality literature **centers near 5** (Hackman & Vidmar 4.6; Hackman 4–6), with a
  defensible band of roughly **4–7** and an upper tolerance to **~10**. **8 is inside the tolerable
  range and inside the two-pizza band, but it is above the research optimum.**
- Therefore an honest citation footing for 8 would read: *"8 sits at the upper bound of the
  supported range (two-pizza 6–8; ≤10 ceiling); decision-quality research centers near 5"* — which
  is **context, not endorsement**.
- **Recommendation: ship the parameter marked *founder-ruled*, with these citations as context —
  not as *research-optimal*.** The literature does not support "8 = optimal"; it supports "~5 =
  optimal, ≤10 = tolerable, 8 = defensible-upper-edge." If the founder wants a research-*optimal*
  number, it is **5** (odd-alternative **7** if an even split must be avoided without a
  supermajority rule). If 8 is kept, pair it with an explicit **supermajority threshold** (e.g.
  6/8) to close the even-deadlock gap.
- This is a reporting memo, not a recommendation to change the parameter — the founder ruling
  stands; the memo only fixes **how** it may honestly be cited.
